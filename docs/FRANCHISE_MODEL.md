# PROSTARS™ — Franchise / Licensing Model

> Companion to `PROSTARS_MASTER_PLAN.md` §7–8. This document is intentionally the least urgent of the seven — it exists so the *option* is well-understood, not because PROSTARS should pursue it in Year 1. It draws on Elanza's own professional experience delivering children's programmes, including with Playball, since that direct view of an established franchise model is valuable competitive intelligence this plan can draw on.

---

## What an established franchise model like Playball's gets right (worth keeping)
- A recognisable national name lowers the trust barrier for a new parent enrolling their child sight-unseen.
- A structured curriculum means quality doesn't depend entirely on one instructor's improvisation.
- Territory-based coach/franchisee assignment prevents two providers competing for the same school hall.

## What it gets wrong (per the founder's direct experience — the actual differentiation)
- **Administrative weight crowds out judgment.** A heavy compliance layer, common to large franchisors, is optimised for consistency-at-scale over instructor discretion. That's a reasonable trade-off for a large franchisor; it's the wrong trade-off for a brand whose whole thesis is "read the child in front of you."
- **No visible developmental throughline for parents.** Sport skills are visible session to session; the deeper "is my child becoming more resilient/confident" story is not surfaced in any structured way. That's precisely the gap PROSTARS's outer-layer proof system (`BRAND_IDENTITY.md`, `WEBSITE_BLUEPRINT.md`) is built to close.
- **Instructor-first, not method-first branding.** A franchise model built around six generic sports (per the Proteus prototype, which followed this pattern too) commoditises the instructor. PROSTARS's Method is the thing being licensed — the instructor's job is to deliver it with judgment, not follow a rigid script.

**Design principle for any future PROSTARS licensing model:** license the *Method and the proof system*, not a rigid session script. Keep the compliance layer as light as legally required (First Aid, police check, child-safety training) and no heavier.

---

## Recommended Structure (not to be activated before Option B territory, per the Master Plan's income scenarios)

### Stage 0 — Single operator (now → Year 1–2)
No franchise structure. Elanza is the only coach. This stage exists to prove the Method actually produces the outer-layer proof it promises, in Elanza's own hands, before anyone else is trusted to deliver it.

### Stage 1 — Employed/contracted second coach (Option B entry point)
Elanza hires or contracts a second coach for a second Canterbury location/timeslot, trained directly by her, paid a wage or session rate — not a franchisee. This is the cheapest way to test "can this Method be taught by someone other than the founder" without the legal and financial complexity of franchising.

### Stage 2 — Certified Coach Licence (regional, Option B/C)
Once Stage 1 proves the Method transfers:
- **PROSTARS Certified Coach** programme: a short, structured certification (built from what Elanza already knows works, not generic sports-coaching content) covering the Method's inner layer, child-safety/First Aid/police-check requirements, and the outer-layer proof/reporting system.
- Licensed coaches operate under the PROSTARS name in a defined Canterbury territory (or, later, another NZ region), paying a **flat monthly licence fee plus a small royalty on revenue** — deliberately simpler than a full franchise-fee structure, reflecting that PROSTARS is licensing a method and a name, not handing over a turnkey business-in-a-box.
- Central PROSTARS retains: the brand, the Method curriculum and its updates, the proof/certificate system (via the Phase 2/3 software), and quality assurance visits.
- Licensed coaches retain: full local relationship ownership (their own school partnerships, their own class scheduling), consistent with the "instructor judgment over script" principle above.

### Stage 3 — Full franchise (Option C, national — years away, evaluate only if Stage 2 works)
Only worth building once there is a proven, documented certification pipeline and at least a handful of successful Stage 2 licensees. At that point, a fuller franchise agreement (franchise fee, marketing fund, formal territory protection) becomes viable — but should still preserve the lighter compliance philosophy that is this brand's actual point of differentiation from Playball.

## What makes PROSTARS franchisable (or not) — honest assessment
**In favour:**
- The two-layer Method (`PROSTARS_MASTER_PLAN.md` §4) is a genuinely teachable, documentable framework, not tacit founder knowledge.
- The proof-and-certificate system, once built in Phase 2, is inherently replicable software — a big part of what a franchisee actually needs.
- Sport Canterbury/Tū Manawa-style regional funding relationships are a repeatable channel-opening playbook other coaches could follow.

**Against, honestly:**
- The brand's core credibility currently rests on Elanza's personal track record as a children's development specialist. A licensed coach without that background needs the certification programme to be genuinely rigorous, not just a badge — otherwise the brand's authenticity claim (the thing that makes it better than a generic sports franchise) erodes the moment it scales.
- No franchise/licensing revenue should be modelled into Year 1–2 financials. This document is a map for later, not a funding assumption for now.
