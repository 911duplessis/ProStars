# PROSTARS™ — Teacher Launch Kit

**"Ek kort die logo en program... Dis al. Daarna kan alles vloei."**
*(I need the logo and the programme. That's all. Then everything else can flow.)*

This is that. Everything else — the website, the app, the franchise plan — can wait. This document is what you need to run your first class: a logo, a mascot, colours, a t-shirt, a medal, a certificate, a parent pamphlet, and eight weeks of sessions, planned week by week.

The strategy behind PROSTARS (the "PROSTARS METHOD") is still there underneath all of this — it's just not something you need to think about. You don't teach a method. You run a fun class, and the method is already built into it.

**The child should see FUN. The parent should see GROWTH. You should have a SIMPLE system.**

## Before anything else: how we talk about PROSTARS right now

This matters, so it comes first.

Because of the 18-month restriction on using your name commercially after leaving Playball, **nothing in PROSTARS should be publicly attributed to you by name yet** — not on the logo, not on the certificate, not on the pamphlet, not on social media.

**Use this instead, everywhere public-facing:**
> PROSTARS™ — created by a Canterbury child development specialist.

That's it. Warm, true, and says nothing that points back to Playball or to you by name. As the coach in the room, you can simply be "Coach" or "your PROSTARS coach" on certificates and in the pamphlet.

**Also avoid, in any PROSTARS material:**
- Sport words: "ball skills," "sports academy," "training," "coaching drills"
- Anything that sounds like a Playball class name or class structure
- Any direct comparison to Playball, even a positive one

**Use instead:** confidence, movement, readiness, growing, trying, play, adventure.

This isn't just a legal box to tick — it's actually the right voice for PROSTARS anyway. It was never meant to sound like a sports class.

## 1. Logo Requirements

You don't need to design this yourself. This section is what to hand to a designer (or a print shop, or a friend who's good with Canva) so they can make it for you.

**The mark:** a five-point star, drawn with one point left open — like it's still being finished. That's the whole idea of PROSTARS in one picture: every child is a star still becoming who they'll be. It should look hand-drawn-simple, not sharp and corporate — think a warm marker line, not a corporate logo.

**Wordmark:** "PROSTARS" next to or under the star, in a rounded, friendly typeface (not a serious business font — something that could sit comfortably on a kid's t-shirt).

**Colours the logo needs to work in:**
- Full colour (for the pamphlet, banners, the website later)
- One colour, black only (for certificates you print at home, stamps)
- White only, reversed out (for dark t-shirts)

**Sizes it needs to work at:**
- Tiny — a 2cm badge or sticker (the open star alone, no wordmark, still needs to be recognisable)
- Medium — a t-shirt chest print
- Large — a banner or the parent pamphlet cover

**What to ask a designer for (so you own it properly):**
- An SVG file (resizes to any size without going blurry)
- A PNG with a transparent background (for slides, printables)
- A single-colour version (for cheap printing)

**What to avoid:** stretching the star out of shape, adding shiny gradients or shadows, using any colour outside the palette below.

## 2. Meet the Mascot: Pip

Every good children's programme needs one character the kids get excited to see — not a "brand mascot," a friend.

**Pip is a small, young star.** Pip hasn't grown all five points yet — one is still soft and open, just like the logo. That's Pip's whole story, and it's a story you can tell the children on day one:

> "This is Pip. Pip is a star who's still growing — just like you! Every week, Pip tries something new, just like we do."

**What Pip looks like:** a round, friendly star shape (not sharp-pointed — soft, huggable edges), two big simple dot eyes, a small curved smile, warm gold-and-coral colouring. No animal, no superhero cape, nothing complicated. Simple enough that a five-year-old could draw a recognisable version of Pip after seeing it twice.

**How to use Pip in class:**
- "What do you think Pip would try?"
- Pip "visits" each week with a new small challenge
- Pip appears on the sticker chart, the certificate, and the medal
- Pip never talks in a script — you decide what Pip says, in the moment, with your group

Pip is on every printed item in this kit, so children build a relationship with one steady, familiar face across all eight weeks.

## 3. Colours — and why they work for ages 3–8

Young children are still learning to settle their bodies and their attention. Too many bright colours at once is overstimulating before a class even starts. PROSTARS uses a **calm base, with bright colours saved for the moments that matter.**

| Colour | Where it shows up | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Warm Sand** (soft cream) | Backgrounds, the pamphlet, the certificate paper | A calm, non-clinical background — nothing shouts at the child before they've even arrived |
| **Dusk Indigo** (deep blue-purple) | Logo, t-shirt, "settling down" moments | Blue tones help young children feel calm and safe — this is the "arriving and settling" colour |
| **Sunrise Coral** (warm orange-pink) | Movement and play moments, Pip's colouring | Warm and inviting without the over-excitement pure red causes — this is the "let's try something" colour |
| **Meadow Green** | Team activities | Associated with "go" and the outdoors — used for anything done together as a group |
| **Golden Star** | Stickers, medals, certificates **only** | Gold is never a background colour. It only appears when a child has actually achieved something — so gold keeps its meaning instead of becoming just decoration |

**The rule for you as the teacher:** if you're introducing something new or asking children to settle, lean on the blue. If you're celebrating something they did, that's when gold appears. Keeping gold rare is what makes it feel special.

## 4. T-Shirt Concepts

Two shirts, not a whole clothing line — keep this simple and affordable to start.

**1. The Everyday PROSTARS Tee**
Soft sand/cream shirt. Small Open Star logo on the chest. Pip peeking out from the back collar or sleeve. This is what the children wear every week — plain enough to wash and wear repeatedly, still clearly "theirs."

**2. The "I Am A PROSTAR" Tee**
Given out (not bought) at the end of the 8-week programme, as part of the final celebration. Indigo shirt, big friendly gold text on the back — "I AM A PROSTAR" — with the Open Star on the front, small. This shirt is a reward, not a uniform, so it should feel different from the everyday one.

**Practical notes for a designer or supplier:**
- Soft, breathable cotton — children will be moving in these
- Unisex cut, sizes to fit ages 3–8
- Screen print, not iron-on (iron-on peels after a few washes)
- No small attachable parts (buttons, sequins) — choking-hazard risk for this age group
- **Optional, cheaper alternative for team activities:** simple green pinnies/bibs instead of a third shirt — reusable across classes, low cost

## 5. Medal Design

The medal is the big moment at the end of Week 8 — it should feel earned, not handed out.

**Weekly stickers first.** Each week, every child gets one sticker for their star chart (see the 8-week programme below — one sticker design per week's theme). By Week 8, a child's chart has 8 stickers on it. This builds anticipation for the medal without giving it away too early.

**The medal itself:**
- Round, with the Open Star on the front and "I AM A PROSTAR" printed in a ring around the edge
- Ribbon in indigo with a coral stripe
- Pip appears small on the back, alongside the child's completion date

**Two versions, so you can start cheap:**
- **Starter version:** a laminated card medal (print the design, cut round, laminate, thread ribbon through a hole punch) — costs almost nothing and looks great to a five-year-old
- **Upgrade version, once there's budget:** an actual pressed-metal medal from a trophy/medal supplier, using the same design

Don't wait for the metal version to start. The laminated one works.

## 6. Certificate Design

One certificate, given at the very end of the 8 weeks — not one per week (that would make the final one feel less special).

**Layout (A4, landscape):**
- Warm sand background, a simple indigo border
- The Open Star, faint, as a watermark behind the text (not overpowering)
- Child's name, large and friendly, in the centre
- "has completed the PROSTARS 8-Week Confidence Adventure"
- A small row of all 8 week titles underneath, with a tick or star next to each one the child completed ("⭐ I Belong," "⭐ I Can Try," and so on)
- Date, and a signature line marked "Your PROSTARS Coach" (not a name, per the naming note above)
- Pip, small, in a corner

This certificate is something a parent will put on the fridge. Design it for that — simple, warm, and clearly about their specific child, not a generic template.

## 8. The First 8-Week Programme: PROSTARS 8-Week Confidence Adventure™

One theme a week. Each one is simple enough to say out loud to a four-year-old, and each one quietly builds a real skill underneath.

| Week | Theme | What's really being built (for you, not the children) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | ⭐ **I Belong** | Feeling safe in the group — the foundation everything else sits on |
| 2 | ⭐ **I Can Try** | Courage to attempt something new, even if it's hard |
| 3 | ⭐ **I Can Balance** | Body control and calm — noticing what your body is doing |
| 4 | ⭐ **I Can Work Together** | Turn-taking, sharing, working alongside a friend |
| 5 | ⭐ **I Can Solve Problems** | Thinking through a small challenge instead of giving up |
| 6 | ⭐ **I Can Be Brave** | Recovering from a wobble, a loss, or a "not yet" |
| 7 | ⭐ **I Can Lead** | Stepping up, helping a friend, being looked to by the group |
| 8 | ⭐ **I Am A PROSTAR** | Celebrating everything — medal and certificate day |

Each week follows the same five-part shape (below), just with a different theme, game, and story. You don't need to reinvent the session every week — you just change the content inside the same simple structure.

## 9. Session Structure

Every PROSTARS session is 45 minutes and follows the same five parts, in the same order, every single week. Children this age feel safe inside routine — knowing what comes next is part of what makes them relax and try.

| Part | Time | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Welcome Activity** | 5 min | Every child is greeted by name in the "Star Circle." A simple ritual (e.g. a star jump and a wave) that's the same every week. |
| **2. Movement / Play** | 15 min | Active, physical, fun — the main "play" part of the session, built around this week's theme. |
| **3. Inner Readiness Lesson** | 10 min | A short story, question, or quiet moment (with Pip) about this week's idea. Sitting down, calmer pace. |
| **4. Team Activity** | 10 min | A group game that puts the week's idea into practice with friends. |
| **5. Achievement Moment** | 5 min | Every child gets their sticker and one specific, honest sentence of praise — not "great job," but something they actually did ("You waited your turn three times today"). |

### Worked example: Week 1 — "I Belong"

**1. Welcome Activity (5 min) — Star Circle**
Children sit in a circle. Go around and greet each child by name: "Hi [name], Pip is so happy you're here!" Each child does one star jump when their name is called.

**2. Movement/Play (15 min) — Follow Pip**
Simple follow-the-leader game — you (or a helper) play "Pip," and the children copy simple movements (stretch tall like a star, curl up small, wiggle). No winners or losers — just moving together.

**3. Inner Readiness Lesson (10 min) — Pip's Story**
Sit down together. Tell the children: "Pip is a star who's still growing all its points — just like you're still growing too. Pip felt a little shy on the first day, just like some of us might feel today. But Pip looked around and saw lots of new friends." Ask: "What's one thing that made you feel a little braver today?"

**4. Team Activity (10 min) — Pass the Star**
Children sit in a circle and pass a soft star-shaped beanbag around while music plays. Whoever holds it when the music stops says one thing about themselves (favourite colour, favourite animal) — a gentle way to help the group learn each other's names and something small about each other.

**5. Achievement Moment (5 min)**
Each child receives their Week 1 sticker on their star chart. Say one true, specific thing to each child: "You said hello to someone new today," or "You waited so patiently for your turn." Close with: "You belong here. See you next week!"

For Weeks 2–8, keep this same shape — swap the story, the movement game, and the team activity to match that week's theme. The routine staying the same is what makes the class feel safe; the content changing each week is what keeps it exciting.
