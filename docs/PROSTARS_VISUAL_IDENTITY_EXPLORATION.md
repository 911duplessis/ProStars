# PROSTARS™ — Visual Identity Exploration

**Stage:** Brand DNA → **Visual Direction** → Concepts → Selection → Refinement → Production
**You are here:** visual direction. Nothing below is final. The job of this document is to find the *feeling*, not to ship a logo.

The founder's brief for this stage, in her own words: *"Ek is n kleuterskool juffie... ek soek prentjies."* (I'm a kindergarten teacher — I want pictures.) So this is pictures, not a moodboard essay.

---

## Brand DNA (the constraint every direction below has to survive)

The brand must be:
- simple
- warm
- memorable
- child-friendly
- parent-trusted
- scalable into a franchise later, without a redesign

Target age: 3–8 years old. Primary market: Canterbury, New Zealand.

**It must never look like:**
- a sports academy
- Playball
- a school worksheet
- a generic cartoon daycare

**It must feel like:** *every child discovers their own star.*

---

## Three complete brand worlds

Live comparison, built as real vector artwork (not a description) — logo, mascot, palette, and a sample medal for each:

**→ [`docs/three-brand-worlds/index.html`](three-brand-worlds/index.html)**

Send that link to Lal before reading anything else here. The only question worth asking her:

> "Which one feels like something you'd proudly put on a child's shirt?"

Her gut reaction is the signal. Not a considered opinion after reading the rationale below — the first, fast, emotional answer.

### World 1 — ⭐ The Star Adventure
**Feeling:** warm, magical, encouraging. **Visual language:** stars, constellations, a journey of becoming.
- **Logo:** the Open Star — a five-point star with one point deliberately left unclosed, like a constellation still forming.
- **Mascot — Pip:** a young star who hasn't grown their last point yet. Pip's whole personality is "still becoming," which mirrors every child in the room.
- **Palette:** Dusk Indigo, Sunrise Coral, Meadow Green, Golden Star, Warm Sand — calm base, bright colours earned only at achievement moments.
- **Why it's the safe, strong default:** it's what the rest of the brand (Teacher Launch Kit, prototype site, the PROSTARS METHOD's inner/outer layer) was already built around. Picking this world costs nothing in rework.
- **Risk:** stars-and-constellations is a common register for children's brands — it's warm but not the most *distinctive* of the three.

### World 2 — 🌱 The Growth Garden
**Feeling:** nurturing, natural, calm confidence — closer to a modern Montessori feeling than a celebration feeling. **Visual language:** seedlings, leaves, growing at your own pace.
- **Logo:** a star drawn as a five-petal flower, with a small stem and a golden centre.
- **Mascot — Sprout:** a small seedling with a tiny star-shaped bud on top — still growing.
- **Palette:** Moss Green, Sunflower, Terracotta, Sky, Cream — earthier and quieter than World 1.
- **Why it might land better with parents specifically:** "growth" and "nurturing" are exactly the words a parent wants to hear about their child's *emotional* development, more directly than "star" does. It also puts real distance between PROSTARS and any sport-adjacent brand, including Playball.
- **Risk:** could read as closer to a plant nursery or an eco-brand than a children's programme if the mascot and copy don't carry it. Needs Sprout to be doing a lot of the warmth work.

### World 3 — 🧭 The Little Explorers
**Feeling:** energetic, playful, a sense of quest and earned achievement. **Visual language:** badges, maps, patches, a journey with stops along the way.
- **Logo:** a star inside a scalloped badge — reads instantly as "medal," before a child even earns one.
- **Mascot — Scout:** a small explorer already wearing a badge sash with one star pinned on — the other seven weeks are visibly still to come.
- **Palette:** Rust, Mustard, Forest, Navy, Cream — a warmer, more "vintage scout" register than either other world.
- **Why this is the strategist's 80%-favourite:** it's the direction that most naturally extends into everything the founder specifically asked for — medals, certificates, t-shirts, a progress chart — because the *whole metaphor is already a badge system*. Nothing has to be invented to explain why there's a medal at the end; the badge sash already told the story in week one.
- **Risk:** "explorer/adventure" register is the one most likely to accidentally drift toward "sports academy" or "adventure camp" if the badge shapes get too militaristic or merit-badge-scout-troop literal. Needs restraint in refinement — soft edges, not sharp ones (see the sample in the comparison page).

## Further directions (not yet visualised — only pursue if none of the three worlds land)

These are logged so nothing is lost, but building all of them before Lal reacts to the three worlds above would be the mistake this whole exercise is designed to avoid: showing a kindergarten teacher forty options instead of three feelings.

**Other logo directions worth a look, if needed:**
1. The Pathway Star — a star at the end of a single winding line, like a path walked to get there.
2. The Initial Mark — a monogram "PS" built from two simple shapes, most versatile for merchandise/social, least literal.
3. The Handprint Star — a child's handprint with the fingers suggesting a star's points (very literal "made by a child," could feel more craft-project than brand).
4. The Rising Sun-Star — a star rising over a simple horizon line (pairs well with "Sunrise Coral").
5. The Compass Star — a star used as the north point of a simple compass rose (a quieter version of World 3's badge).
6. The Building Blocks Star — a star assembled from a few simple stacked shapes (leans younger, 3–5 skew).
7. The Ribbon Star — a star with a small ribbon/banner beneath, achievement-forward without the full badge treatment.
8. The Footprint Trail — small star-shaped footprints forming a path (playful, but risks reading too literally like "steps," could confuse with a walking/mobility programme).
9. The Nest Star — a star cradled in a simple nest/cup shape (very nurturing, closest cousin to World 2).
10. The Puzzle-Piece Star — a star made of a few interlocking puzzle pieces ("every child is a piece that fits" — strong for group/belonging messaging, weaker as a standalone mark).

**Other mascot directions worth a look:**
1. A round "buddy" blob character with no specific identity (maximally flexible, least memorable).
2. A small bird just learning to fly (confidence/courage metaphor, well-worn territory in children's media).
3. A turtle who comes out of its shell a little more each week (resilience/shyness metaphor, slower-energy feel).
4. A firefly whose light grows brighter each week (achievement-as-light metaphor, very literal for the "star" name).
5. A cloud that's learning to make its own sunshine (emotional-regulation metaphor, abstract — may be hard for a 3-year-old to parse).
6. A kite still learning to fly steady (resilience/trying metaphor, seasonal/NZ-outdoors resonance).
7. A paper boat on a small journey (adventure metaphor without World 3's "badge" specificity).
8. A pair of mismatched shoes/socks who become a matching pair (belonging/friendship metaphor, very literal for "I Can Work Together").
9. A jigsaw-piece character (see puzzle-piece logo above — could be the mascot instead of the mark).
10. No single mascot — icon-only system, badges and stars carry the personality instead of a character (lowest cost, least emotionally sticky with young children — not recommended, included for completeness).

**Other colour palette directions:**
1. **Twilight & Firefly** — deep navy base with a single glowing gold accent, night-sky feeling (very "star," possibly too dim/serious for a 3-year-old's daytime class).
2. **Playground Bright** — primary-adjacent but softened (coral, sky blue, sunshine yellow) — closest to "typical kids' brand," which is exactly the risk: it's the look most likely to blur into every other programme.
3. **Sand & Sea** — Canterbury-coastal palette (driftwood, sea-glass green, soft blue, shell pink) — strong local-identity angle, worth testing if "made in Canterbury" becomes a bigger part of the pitch.
4. **Harvest Warm** — closer to World 3 but pushed further into autumn/harvest tones (pumpkin, wheat, deep red) — very NZ-seasonal, risks feeling more "farmers market" than "children's programme."
5. **Single-Accent Neutral** — mostly warm neutrals (sand, cream, soft grey) with exactly one bright accent colour reserved for achievement — the most premium/Montessori-adjacent option, least "fun" at first glance.

**Certificate style directions:**
1. Landscape A4 with a large illustrated scene behind the text (most visually rich, priciest to produce well).
2. A "passport" style booklet — one stamp per week instead of one certificate at the end (matches World 3 especially well; more production cost, more ongoing delight).
3. A single bold word ("BRAVE." "TRYING." "BELONGING.") as the dominant visual, name and details small underneath (very modern, very shareable on social, less "traditional certificate" for a fridge).
4. A hand-lettered, almost hand-made-looking certificate (warmest, most "made by a real teacher" feeling — harder to keep consistent at scale).
5. A fold-out card — certificate on the inside, a short personal note space on the front (turns it into a keepsake, not just a document).

**Medal/badge system directions:**
1. One medal only, at the end (what's already in the Teacher Launch Kit — simplest to produce and manage).
2. A badge per week, sewn or stuck onto a sash/board, with no separate final medal — the badges *are* the achievement record (fits World 3 perfectly, needs a sash/board object to exist).
3. A single medal with interchangeable weekly charms/pins added to it over the eight weeks (higher production complexity, high delight, souvenir-grade).
4. Digital-only badges (a QR code on the certificate linking to a shareable digital badge image for social media) — cheap, modern, but loses the "something to hold" the founder specifically asked for.
5. A "growing" medal — a design that's visibly incomplete week to week (matches World 1/2's growth metaphor; harder to manufacture cheaply than a flat badge).

**T-shirt concept directions:**
1. What's already in the Teacher Launch Kit: an everyday tee + a reward tee for programme completion.
2. A single tee with a small tally/tracker element (e.g. a row of tiny stars) that gets ticked off in fabric marker week by week — turns the shirt itself into the progress chart.
3. Removable iron-on weekly patches on one reusable base shirt — most cost-effective for parents, most "collectible" for kids.
4. A plain shirt with only a small chest mark, no back print at all — the most premium/understated option, works well if PROSTARS ever wants a more Montessori-adult-trusted register.
5. Bright team-colour shirts by class/cohort rather than one universal design — useful only once there are multiple concurrent classes to visually distinguish.

**Classroom environment ideas:**
- A single portable "world" banner or floor mat (star field / garden / map, depending on world chosen) that turns any borrowed school hall into "PROSTARS space" for the hour, then packs away.
- A rotating physical prop tied to the week's theme (e.g. a real small plant that grows week to week for World 2; a class "map" that gets a new sticker each week for World 3).
- A consistent opening/closing ritual object (see the Teacher Launch Kit's "Star Circle") — the one thing that's identical every single week regardless of which world's visuals surround it.
- A parent-facing corner or board at pickup — even just a clipboard — showing that week's theme and one photo, so the visual identity is doing marketing work in the room itself, not just on the shirt.

## What happens after Lal reacts

1. She picks the one that gets an actual emotional response — "Ja, dis cute," not a considered essay.
2. That world's logo, mascot, and palette become canonical, replacing whatever's currently in `BRAND_IDENTITY.md` and the Teacher Launch Kit if it isn't already World 1.
3. Vector concepts here move into Canva as real, editable templates (flyers, certificates, social posts, the parent pamphlet) — production, not exploration.
4. Only once that's done does a professional designer get engaged, if at all — to convert the chosen concept into a polished, print-ready production identity. Not before the concept is chosen.
