# PROSTARS™ — Logo Exploration

**Direction locked:** ⭐ The Star Adventure. This document does not propose new brand worlds — it explores five ways to *execute* the one already chosen.

**Working visual:** `docs/logo-exploration/index.html`

The logo's job is bigger than "look nice." It has to represent PROSTARS™ itself, the Star Journey age system, real developmental stages, real achievement, warmth, and enough trust that a parent hands over money on the strength of it — and it has to be something a child actually wants on their shirt. A generic cute star clears none of that bar except the last one. Every direction below was tested against the whole list, not just the last item.

It also has to physically work in five places: a website, a child's t-shirt, a medal, a certificate, and — eventually — signage at a second location or a partner school. A mark that only works at hero-image size isn't a logo yet, it's an illustration.

---

## Direction 1 — The Growing Star

**Concept:** The mark already in use everywhere: a five-point star drawn with one point left open, like a constellation still forming.

**Meaning:** This is the whole PROSTARS thesis compressed into one shape — every child is a star still becoming who they'll be. It's also structurally honest: it's the exact same vector path that powers the Star Journey system (§5 of `PROSTARS_STAR_ADVENTURE_BRAND_SYSTEM.md`), just drawn to completion instead of partially. One idea, one asset, doing two jobs.

**Strengths:**
- Already built, already live across every existing document and prototype — zero rework cost.
- Reads clearly at every size tested, from a 2cm badge to a banner.
- The open point is a genuine, ownable piece of narrative IP (see the callout at the end of this document).

**Weaknesses:**
- On its own, "star with a gap" is a register other children's brands could plausibly land near — it's warm but not maximally distinctive as a static image. The Star Journey system is what makes it distinctive; the logo alone is merely good.

**Scalability:** High. This is the mark that has to survive a second location, a franchise, a school partnership — and it already does, because it doesn't depend on a mascot's specific personality or a joke that ages badly.

**Applications:**
- **T-shirt:** small chest print, coral outline on sand — already specified in the Teacher Launch Kit.
- **Medal:** the outline itself becomes the tiered badge system — 25% / 55% / 82% / fully closed gold, per age stage.
- **Certificate:** faint full-size watermark behind the text, at whatever completion percentage matches the child's current tier.

**Recommendation: primary mark.** Everything else in this document is either a variant of this idea (Direction 5) or a deliberately different tool for a specific job (Directions 2–4).

## Direction 2 — Pip the Star Mascot

**Concept:** Pip's simple face — two dot eyes, a curved smile — set directly inside the open star outline. Logo and mascot merged into a single compact mark.

**Meaning:** Where Direction 1 is the brand's *promise*, this is the brand's *friend*. It's the version a three-year-old recognises fastest, because it has a face.

**Strengths:**
- Warmest option by a wide margin — the face does real emotional work that an abstract star can't.
- Doubles as Pip's own portrait mark for anywhere Pip needs a single-image representation (a sticker, an app icon later, a social avatar).

**Weaknesses:**
- Too informal for anything that needs to read as credible to an adult meeting PROSTARS for the first time — a school principal, a prospective franchisee, an investor. A face-logo skews younger and cuter than the "premium, parent-trusted" half of the brand personality wants by default.
- Doesn't scale down as cleanly as Direction 1 — the face details are the first thing to blur out at very small sizes (a 1cm badge, a favicon).

**Scalability:** Medium. Excellent as a *secondary* mark; risky as the only mark, because it locks the brand's most formal touchpoints to a mascot rather than an idea.

**Applications:**
- **T-shirt:** works nicely as an alternate chest print for younger age bands specifically (Little Star, Growing Star) where warmth should outrank formality.
- **Medal:** viable, though it starts to compete visually with the tier-completion mechanic (a face doesn't obviously communicate "82% grown" the way an outline does).
- **Certificate:** best used small, in the Pip-in-the-corner position already specified in the Teacher Launch Kit — not as the main watermark.

**Recommendation: secondary/icon mark.** Use for app icons, favicons, and anything young-child-facing and informal. Never the mark on a formal document, a school proposal, or a franchise agreement.

## Direction 3 — Star + Path

**Concept:** A short trail of steps (dots on a dashed line) rising toward a small solid star.

**Meaning:** The most literal "journey" visual of the five — it shows movement toward the star rather than the star itself changing.

**Strengths:**
- Communicates "progress over time" instantly and needs no explanation — useful specifically for a progress chart, a parent-dashboard mockup, or a future app screen where "journey" needs to be shown alongside other UI, not stand alone as branding.

**Weaknesses:**
- Weakest as a standalone logo of the five. A path-plus-star composition needs real room to read clearly; compressed to badge or favicon size, the dots and star blur into noise.
- Doesn't reuse any existing asset — it's the one direction here that would be pure net-new production cost.

**Scalability:** Low as a primary mark, situationally useful as a UI element once Phase 2/3 (the website and app) exist.

**Applications:**
- **T-shirt:** not recommended — too fussy for fabric print at the sizes a child's shirt allows.
- **Medal:** not recommended for the same reason Direction 1's tiered stars already do this job better.
- **Certificate:** genuinely useful here as a small progress-chart element alongside the main watermark, not as a replacement for it.

**Recommendation: shelved for now.** Revisit specifically when `WEBSITE_BLUEPRINT.md`'s parent dashboard or `APP_ROADMAP.md`'s progress screens get built — this is UI iconography, not a logo.

## Direction 4 — Premium Education Mark

**Concept:** The same closed star shape as Direction 1, but stripped down: thin single-weight outline, one colour (indigo), no gold, no mascot warmth.

**Meaning:** This is PROSTARS with the volume turned down — the version built for a reader who needs "credible institution" to land before "warm and playful" does.

**Strengths:**
- The only direction of the five explicitly built for the "school partnerships" and "future franchise" requirements in the brief. A principal deciding whether to hand over a hall for a term is reading a different signal than a five-year-old choosing a shirt.
- Ages well — a restrained mark doesn't date the way a very "of-the-moment" cute mascot style can.

**Weaknesses:**
- On its own, loses almost everything that makes PROSTARS *feel* like PROSTARS — no warmth, no story, no open point telling the growth narrative. It's a fallback register, not a replacement for the primary mark.

**Scalability:** High, but narrow — it scales well precisely because it does less.

**Applications:**
- **T-shirt:** not intended for children's shirts at all — this is an adult-facing document mark.
- **Medal:** not applicable — medals are for children, this mark is for adults making a decision.
- **Certificate:** works well as a formal seal/stamp variant if PROSTARS ever needs a version that reads as "official" rather than "warm" (e.g. a franchise certification document, distinct from a child's completion certificate).

**Recommendation: reserved variant.** Keep it built and ready, but do not let it become the default anywhere a parent or child is the primary audience.

## Direction 5 — Badge Star

**Concept:** The star set inside a scalloped, medal-shaped badge edge.

**Meaning:** This isn't a logo pretending to be a medal — it's honest about being a medal shape from the start. Reads as "award" the instant it's seen, before any text is read.

**Strengths:**
- Instant recognisability for its actual purpose. No other direction communicates "you earned this" as fast.
- Already effectively in production use — this is close to how the PROSTAR-tier (age 8) completed medal already works in the Star Journey system.

**Weaknesses:**
- Too heavy and too specific a shape to work as the everyday logo — a badge on every pamphlet and every page of a website starts to feel like everything is an award, which cheapens the real ones.

**Scalability:** High, but purpose-bound — it will always be "the medal mark," which is exactly right and exactly limiting.

**Applications:**
- **T-shirt:** only appropriate for the completion tee (see `PROSTARS_STAR_ADVENTURE_BRAND_SYSTEM.md` §8) — never the everyday tee.
- **Medal:** this is the medal. Direct production-ready shape for the physical medal described in the Teacher Launch Kit and Brand System docs.
- **Certificate:** works as a corner seal/stamp on the certificate, alongside — not instead of — the main open-star watermark.

**Recommendation: medal-specific mark.** Formalises what the Star Journey system already implies. Do not promote it to general logo use.

## Recommendation summary

| Direction | Role |
|---|---|
| 1. The Growing Star | **Primary logo** — everywhere |
| 2. Pip the Star Mascot | Secondary icon — app icon, favicon, young-child-facing informal use |
| 3. Star + Path | Shelved — revisit for website/app UI, not as a logo |
| 4. Premium Education Mark | Reserved — schools, franchise paperwork, adult-facing formal documents only |
| 5. Badge Star | Medal-specific — physical medals and certificate seals only |

No new decision is required from the founder here beyond confirming this hierarchy feels right. Direction 1 is already what she approved when she picked "Star een" — this document exists to make sure the other four directions get used *correctly* rather than getting used interchangeably by accident later.

## The idea worth protecting

Most children's brands say some version of *"your child is a star."* It's a compliment, and it stops there.

PROSTARS can say something a compliment can't:

> **"Every child starts as a star. Our programme helps them discover how brightly they can shine."**

That's not a tagline decoration — it's a mechanism. The star is deliberately unfinished at the start, and closes, visibly, through real effort over real years. No other direction explored here — and no competitor identified in `PROSTARS_MASTER_PLAN.md`'s market research — has a logo that is also literally the progress-tracking system. That combination (Direction 1 + the Star Journey) is the single strongest piece of brand IP produced so far. It should be protected as such: kept consistent, not diluted by swapping in Direction 2, 3, or 5 as the "main" logo out of convenience later.
